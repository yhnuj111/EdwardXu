      Module 1 /Contacting Bodies (2 of 2)

      Learning Objectives

      • Identify attributes of forces acting between frictionless contacting bodies.
      Example
      Contacting Bodies 3

      In the third example let us consider two blocks resting on a flat table in contact with one another. Although they touch one another, we assume that the bodies do not adhere (are not glued) to each other, and that the contacting surfaces of the blocks are perfectly smooth (slippery). To convey the slipperiness of the surfaces in the language of mechanics, we say the frictional interaction between the two bodies is negligible.

      Imagine you are to grasp block B in your hand and use it to cause motion of block A in various directions.

      Could you cause block A to move to the left? to the right? up? down? What does this tell you about the directions and sense of the possible interaction between A and B?

      Observation: Block A cannot be moved parallel to its contact surface with block B, because these surfaces are smooth (frictionless).

      Body B moves up or down, but body A stays in place. This means that there is no force acting on A, and therefore there is no interaction between A and B.

      Observation: Body B moves to the right, separating from A, and body A stays in place. This means that there is no force acting on A, and therefore there is no interaction between A and B. Since body A and B do not stick to each other, they cannot pull on each other. The force on each of the two bodies cannot point outward from the body.

      Conclusion: Frictionless contacting bodies cannot exert forces on one another in the direction tangent to the area of contact; the direction of the force between them must be perpendicular to their common surface. Contacting frictionless bodies cannot pull on each other (unless they are glued together); they can exert only forces acting toward each other.

      To Sum Up...
      • Contact forces that are uniformly distributed over the contacting surfaces may be represented by concentrated forces acting at the geometric center of the distribution.
      • The interaction between two frictionless contacting bodies has the following attributes:
        • point of application - at the center of the area of uniform contact
        • direction - both forces act in the direction normal to the contacting surface
        • sense - force on each body is toward that body; bodies push on one another, exerting forces toward one another, but cannot pull on each other
        • magnitude - both forces have the same unknown magnitude, labeled N sometimes with subscript(s) denoting one or both bodies
